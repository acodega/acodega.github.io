In December, I was excited to finally install four Chrome for Meeting systems at my employer's Providence office.

Being a G Suite shop, Upserve has always used Hangouts video calls for video conferencing between employees. Over time with the introduction of features [like easier guest access](https://gsuiteupdates.googleblog.com/2015/11/guest-access-to-hangouts-video-calls.html), [meeting room devices](https://enterprise.google.com/chrome/video-conferencing/), and [joining from a smartphone](https://www.google.com/mobile/hangouts/), our use of Hangouts became even more prevalent.

## About Chrome for Meetings

Chrome for Meetings is Google's line of meeting appliances allowing participants to join a Hangouts call in a meeting room without needing their computer. Via a $249/year license, the device becomes a resource in your G Suite organization and can be booked by inviting it to meetings manually or automatically if the device is assigned to a meeting room. Book the meeting room and the appliance automatically knows about it. Use the remote to join a scheduled call or start one on demand. The appliances are available in two forms, a traditional Chromebox with USB camera and speaker microphone to connect to an HDTV, or a Chromebase touchscreen with built-in camera and speaker microphone meant for small huddle rooms and phone booths.

When Chrome for Meetings first came out, we purchased the [standard room kit](https://enterprise.google.com/chrome/devices/asus-chromebox-for-meetings/) and installed it on a HDTV cart as a trial. We had employees working from home often but the push for great remote collaboration tools wasn't really there so we wanted to see what call adoption was like by purchasing the cart. It quickly became a hotly used resource, however it wasn't selling Chrome for Meetings properly. You had to plan to get the cart before your meeting, the screen was only 50", and the speaker microphone was decent but had issues because it was constantly being picked up and put down. (It connected via USB) 

Two years later with the addition of our San Francisco office and an outside sales team the need for proper in room video conferencing was obvious. In fact some of the most excited employees were those who wouldn't even be using the devices in the office, but would be on the other end of the call.

## Picking out the equipment
Choosing the right screen, camera, and audio option was going to make this project a success.

While Google offers an upgraded video experience in its large room kit with the [Logitech PTZ Pro Camera](http://www.logitech.com/en-us/product/conferencecam-ptz-pro), it tries to get away with sub par audio, simply including two Jabra speaker microphones. The Jabras are nice to use with one's laptop but it's not proper in-room audio.

The thing with Chromeboxes is they can accept any USB device and it will probably work. However, we had issues when we tried using on hand [ClearOne Chat 160s](http://www.clearone.com/products_chat160), the audio would cut out randomly. I contacted the support team at [Conference Room Systems](http://www.conferenceroomsystems.com/Default.asp) and asked what devices they knew would work with Chromeboxes. They said they'd get back to me and later on that day they gave me some options, including the [Pheonix Audio Spider MT 503](http://www.phnxaudio.com/spider/mt503/) which we ended up purchasing. While it connects via USB, it has an [optional MT340 power hub](http://www.phnxaudio.com/accessories/mt340/). The power hub connects to th Chromebox with USB and then plugs into an outlet and allows you to run a network cable to your Spider for both power and connectivity. Up to 15 Spiders can daisy chain to each other also via network cable, this worked out perfectly for the installation.

With the audio picked out, next was deciding on a display. You really can't go too large in a conference room, especially since it will be used for screen sharing. (Which we'll get into later) I found that shopping for anything larger than 60" meant you were getting into home theater territory with features and qualities you don't need for video conferencing. We just needed a large, well priced 1080p display, the dumber the better. We eventually found the [Vizio D70-D3 70" 1080p HDTV](https://www.vizio.com/d70d3.html) for less than $1000. We bought the rest of the equipment we needed, HDMI cables, TV mount, network cables, on Amazon.

## Presenting to the room
Half of what we wanted to accomplish with this project was fixing the experience of trying to present from your computer to the meeting room. We had Apple TVs with projectors and they just don't work, plus even if they did there wouldn't be a convenient way to combine AirPlaying with a video call. We've simply given up on the Apple TVs and now use Hangouts screensharing to present to the room and to the video call.

This sounds a little hard to follow in a blog post, but if you're in a meeting room on a video call and want to present your screen to the video call (and thus the screen too), just goto https://g.co/present in your web browser. This joins you to the video call without enabling your camera or microphone and shares your screen with one click. The experience has worked really well so far and the resolution stays crisp with the help of the 70" HDTV. 

What if you aren't on a video call but want to present to people in the room with you? Google makes a video call for every calendar event someone makes, so as long as the room is booked there's a video call to join. Join the video call on the screen, and goto the present URL above and start the screen share.

If there isn't an event booked in the room you can start one from the remote control, and if you're not invited to the event you can simply enter the event's name (displayed on the meeting room screen) at the Present URL.

## Legacy connections
Of course, we still wanted to offer a wired option as a backup, so we installed HDMI on the table top of the meeting room table which connected to the HDTV. I found it ironic that half of the time spent installing the equipment was used fishing HDMI between the table and the HDTV screen, and a good amount of the budget was used to purchase the [table top power and HDMI box](https://www.conferenceroomav.com/Table_Top_Boxes-c/Surface_Mount).
